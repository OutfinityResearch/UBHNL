ANF (Algebraic Normal Form)

Algebraic Normal Form: every Boolean function has a unique representation as a GF(2) polynomial.

DRAFT: Wiki page.
Wiki Theory GF(2)

Definition (minimal form)

For Boolean variables x1..xn, a function f:{0,1}^n → {0,1} can be written as: 

f(x) = ⊕_{S ⊆ {1..n}} a_S · ∏_{i∈S} x_i
unde a_S ∈ {0,1}

This is ANF. “⊕” is XOR (addition modulo 2), and multiplication is AND.

How it connects to UBH

  • A UBH circuit (XOR/AND) can be viewed as evaluating a GF(2) polynomial (an implicit ANF).
  • Conversely, an ANF polynomial compiles directly to UBH by building monomials (AND) and summing them (XOR).

Why it is useful (engineering)

  • Explains why XOR constraints are natural and why they deserve native treatment in the solver (XOR linear).
  • Provides a bridge between “logic” and “algebra”: some optimizations are algebraic rewrites.

Limits / caveats

  • ANF is unique, but it can be large for some functions; UBH prefers a DAG + hash-consing for structural compression.
  • “Linear ANF” (no degree > 1 terms) is exactly the XOR-linear fragment (solvable by Gauss).
