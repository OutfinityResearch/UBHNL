Scientific Theories and Peer Review

Represent claims formally and check consistency across papers and experiments.

What this enables

  • Formal claims: hypotheses and empirical constraints written in CNL/DSL.
  • Consistency checks: detect contradictions within a paper or across sources.
  • Traceable review: evidence links to specific statements and data constraints.

Workflow (practical)

  1. Define domain vocabulary for the field (variables, units, categories).
  2. Encode claims, assumptions, and constraints in CNL/DSL.
  3. Run entailment/refutation queries to validate logical consistency.
  4. Report contradictions and missing assumptions with origins.

Outputs you can expect

  • Proofs or counterexamples for claims.
  • Conflict sets across multiple sources.
  • Explicit dependency graphs (which assumptions support which results).

Limits and constraints

  • Numerical models require explicit modeling choices and fragments.
  • Ambiguous claims must be rewritten into precise CNL.
  • Large domains may require schema tactics or approximations with audit metadata.

What we still need

  • Libraries for units, measurements, and experimental schemas.
  • Connectors to data repositories for reproducible evidence.
  • Peer-review workflows and templates for consistent encoding.
