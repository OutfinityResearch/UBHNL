The Use Case: The Courtroom

Imagine a courtroom. A prosecutor stands up and says: "The defendant is guilty." The judge doesn't just say "Okay" and close the case. The judge demands Evidence and a Logical Argument that connects that evidence to the crime.

In UBHNL, our Solver is the Judge. When it claims that a set of rules is impossible (UNSAT), it must provide a Certificate—a step-by-step proof that anyone can verify to ensure no mistakes were made.

The Three Answers: SAT, UNSAT, and UNKNOWN

Most software gives you "True" or "False". UBHNL is more honest. It has three possible responses:

  • SAT (Satisfiable): "I found a way to make this work. Here is the Witness (a model) to prove it."
  • UNSAT (Unsatisfiable): "This is mathematically impossible. Here is the Certificate (the proof) that shows the contradiction."
  • UNKNOWN: "I don't know yet. Maybe the problem is too hard, or I ran out of time, or I'm using a method that isn't precise enough."

The Hybrid Engine: SAT + XOR

How does the "Judge" reach a verdict so quickly? By using two specialized specialists:

  1. The SAT Specialist: Great at handling "If-Then" rules and complex dependencies using a method called CDCL.
  2. The XOR Specialist: A math whiz who handles linear equations over bits (GF(2)) using Gaussian Elimination.
By letting these two specialists talk to each other, UBHNL can solve problems that would be hundreds of times larger if we only used one method.

Explainability: Why did this happen?

If the system finds a contradiction, it can pinpoint exactly which rules caused the problem. This is called an Unsat Core. For our doctor, Elena, this means the system can say: "Your rules are contradictory because Rule A says 'Fever is required' but Rule B says 'Fever is impossible' for this specific patient."

Limits & Future: The Quest for Better Proofs

What we have: A system that returns certificates for its claims, ensuring that we never have to "blindly trust" the solver.

What we're building: Human-Readable Proofs. Currently, our certificates are in machine-readable formats like DRAT or LRAT. They are great for computers, but hard for humans. We are working on a Proof Translator that turns these mathematical traces back into Natural Language stories.
