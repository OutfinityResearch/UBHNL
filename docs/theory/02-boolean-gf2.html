Theory 02 — Bit, GF(2), ANF

Why XOR/AND are sufficient, and how GF(2) polynomials show up in practice.

DRAFT: tutorial chapter.

1) Everything starts with a bit

In UBHNL, everything ultimately lowers to a bit: {0,1}. “Truth” is just a value in {0,1} under a chosen interpretation (a model).

2) GF(2): the algebraic interpretation

Over GF(2) (the field with two elements): XOR is addition modulo 2, and AND is multiplication. 

a ⊕ b  ↔  a + b (mod 2)
a ∧ b  ↔  a · b

3) Why {XOR, AND, 1} is a complete basis

Negation is “free”: ¬x = 1 ⊕ x. OR can also be derived: 

x ∨ y = x ⊕ y ⊕ (x ∧ y)

Engineering consequence: the kernel can remain tiny (UBH), while “rich operators” (OR, implication, arithmetic, comparisons) can live in libraries as macros compiled down to XOR/AND/CONST.

4) ANF: every Boolean function as a polynomial

ANF says: every Boolean function has a unique representation as a GF(2) polynomial (a multilinear polynomial under the Boolean identities). This is why UBH is naturally “polynomial-friendly”.

5) Why XOR deserves native treatment

  • XOR constraints form a linear fragment that can be solved efficiently with Gaussian elimination.
  • Encoding XOR into pure CNF can be expensive; hence the SAT+native-XOR decision.
